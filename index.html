Church Patronage Trust

Why Patrons?

Patronage is an historic element in the Church of England carried over from the pre-Reformation Church. Each parish has a patron who in the event of a vacancy exercises the right to present a candidate for the benefice of that parish. Patrons desire, under God, to act in the best interest of the parishes they represent in order to ensure that right appointments are made in the parish and the Kingdom of God is established on firm biblical and pastoral foundations.

The Church Patronage Trust

The Church Patronage Trust, established in 1871, exercises sole patronage of 64 parishes and joint patronage of 61 parishes. It is one of the larger evangelical patronage bodies and most of its parishes are located in the Midlands and South of England. CPT seeks to support the local church and its work is focussed on strengthening the evangelistic/pastoral ministry exercised in the parish. The Appointment Process

The process of appointment of clergy is best seen as a 'three-legged stool', involving two parish representatives (lay members of the PCC), the Diocese (Bishop/Archdeacon) and the Patron. Technically, the Patron nominates, the parish agree to the nomination and the Bishop institutes. In reality all three parties openly discuss/interview the candidates and all three parties must agree to the final decision. We count it a huge privilege to work alongside Dioceses and parishes in this way.

When a vacancy arises in one of the CPT parishes, ideally one of the Trustees/Assessors/Secretary visits the PCC to explain the appointment process and the Patron's role. This often occurs at what is termed a Section 12 meeting when representatives from the Diocese and the Patrons meet with the PCC. At this meeting the parish profile will be discussed together with the anticipated time scale for advertising.

At CPT we always encourage the advertising of posts followed by short listing and a day of interviews in the parish. Thus we seek to engage all parties to discern the mind of God in an open way.

Suspension of Presentation

If, to use the technical language, 'presentation is suspended' because of the possibility of pastoral reorganisation involving the parish, then, instead of a Vicar or Rector being instituted, a 'Priest in Charge' will be licensed. Although the terms of appointment are different, the Parishioners will not notice the difference and will view the Minister as their Vicar/Rector. In this case, we still value the opportunity to adopt the 'three-legged stool' procedure to ensure each party feels fully involved in the appointment. Following the introduction of 'Common Tenure' has been a reduction in the number of 'suspensions'. It is important to note the recommendation that 'Common Tenure leaves the rights of Patrons unchanged'.
